February 19th, 1867 George W. Black to Judge W. A. Carter

Chicago Ills [Illinois] Feby [February] 19th, 1867 Judge 1W.A. Carter Esq Washington D.C.

Dear Sir,

Since your departure some of my Chicago friends have presented me with certificates of character, they having known me many years. They said that they would do no hurt to have them with me.

I have also written to Samuel Deanin Salt Lake, for like certificate. I having employed with him in the Quartermaster Department as clerk, at the place. I also wrote to Webb & Brother, freighters in that place. They employed me when I first went into the country for several months, and knew my reputation while there.

I directed them to send the certificate to you at Ft. Bridger, thinking you would be likely to get them there, as I may be taken west soon to recieve them here.

Mr. Yates came to day and took me out to visit my wife and some other friends. I think him very kind, and a perfect gentleman.

2Stickney told some of the prisoners that he had money enough to get him out of this scrape. Though I do not fewar that he will accomplish what he effects to.

I have all the faith that any one possible could have in yourself and Mr. Yates, and thinkg that I shall have justice done, and come home exhonorated [exonerated] from all blame, for God knows that the thought of committing a wicked crime was never in my heart. Words would not induce me to do anything that would brake [break] a wife's heart and bring any aged mother in sorrow to her grave. Pray excuse me for writing such sentiments but I cannot help it.

Allow me to thank you for the kind interest you have manifested, for which I shall be indebted to you.

Any word from you will gladly be recieved through Mr. Yates at this place.

With Respect



Your obedient servant,



3George W. Black

George W. Black and M.S. Stickney were accused of murdering a Mr. Bicking on October 19th 1866 while all three men were traveling together near Salt Lake City. Black at the time of writing this letter had been detained in the Cook County Jail in Chicago.

1. Judge William A. Carter was born in 1818 in Virginia. In 1857 he moved to Fort Bridger primarily to become a trader. In 1867 Judge Carter was contacted by Black, Mrs. Bicking, and Henry Stanbery primarily for his influence as a businessman and Probate Judge in Unita County in Wyoming and was central to the economic activity in Fort Bridger ↩ 2. M.S. Stickney was a man of wealthy or connected means and travel companion to George W. Black. It is believed Stickney coerced Black to shoot and kill Bicking while the group was all traveling together to Salt Lake City, and he was able to get an early release from the Cook County jail and later sue habeas corpus due to unlawful imprisonment. ↩ 3. George W. Black was a trader and frontiersman, likely from a wealthy family. On October 16, 1866 while traveling with Stickney and Bicking near Salt Lake City Black shot and killed Bicking at the prompting of Stickney. The exact reason why is unknown, but Black admitted to commiting the murder even before his arrest in early 1867. Black reached out to Judge Carter in an attempt to secure a character witness, without further elaboration on the details of how they know each other. ↩
